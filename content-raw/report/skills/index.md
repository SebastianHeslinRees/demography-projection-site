Across the 2015-2024 time series, the proportion of London’s working age population without qualifications shows a moderate downward trend. Although the latest figures for London show a small rise from 5.2% to 5.6% between 2023 and 2024, the proportion remains well below the 2015 level of 7.4%. The UK follows the same overall pattern, but throughout the whole time period it has consistently reported a higher share of people without qualifications than London.

Correspondingly, the proportion of working-age Londoners with higher level qualifications has been increasing over time. The latest data shows that more than three-quarters (76%) of 16-64 year-old Londoners hold a Level 3 or above qualification, higher than for the UK overall (68%).

<img src="attachments/skills/media/image6.svg" style="width:6.26389in;height:3.13194in" />

The latest available data on labour market outcomes for London’s FE learners show the outcomes for Londoners who either completed education and training courses or apprenticeships. The share of 2022/23 (academic year) adult London learners recorded as having a Sustained Positive Destination (SPD), which includes economic outcomes and further learning for at least a 6-month period in the following year (2023/24), was 72%. This is below the England average of 77%, and the gap has widened since the previous year, when London learners had an SPD rate of 74% compared with 76% for England.

Variation in SPD rates over time are driven mainly by changes in sustained employment, as rates of sustained learning and the mix of sustained learning and employment have been relatively stable over time. In 2020/21, the SPD rate reached 78%, reflecting a notable jump in sustained employment. One cause potentially driving that high sustained employment rate was the high number of vacancies following the pandemic, which indicates strong employer demand. In the latest figures, sustained employment has decreased again, leading overall SPD rates to return to levels similar to those seen before the pandemic.

The latest available data shows that 86% of London HEI graduates in academic year 2020/21 were in a positive destination (i.e., either employment or further study) the year after graduating. This is a marginal decline compared to the previous year (88%). The proportion is slightly lower (by 4 percentage points in 2020/21) than for English HEIs overall. This is due to London having a more competitive labour market, with a higher share of the workforce having degree qualifications or higher and London attracting international talent, making it comparatively more difficult for new graduates to enter the labour market.

<img src="attachments/skills/media/image10.svg" style="width:6.26389in;height:3.19306in" />

Between 2015 and 2022, the percentage of employees who are judged as ‘not fully proficient at their job’ by their employer, also called the skills gap density, has remained relatively stable, with a small rise in 2022 (5% in 2015 and 5.8% in 2022). A similar trend and proportions were seen across England. In 2024, for London, the skills gap density declined to 5.6%, however this was still higher than the pre-pandemic percentage of 3.8% and only marginally below the 2022 peak. At the England level the skills gap declined to below the pre-pandemic level in 2024, and remains below the 2019 share.

<img src="attachments/skills/media/image12.svg" style="width:6.26389in;height:3.19306in" />

‘Skills shortage vacancies’ refer to vacancies that employers struggle to fill due to a lack of skills, qualifications or experience amongst applicants. The share of skills shortage vacancies, amongst all vacancies in London, increased significantly between 2015 and 2022, from 20% to 32%. In 2022, the share of skills shortage vacancies was 4 percentage points below the share across all of England. Data for 2024 shows some easing, with the share of skills shortage vacancies in London declining to 24% but remaining above the 2019 level of 21%, a similar trend was seen at the England level.

<img src="attachments/skills/media/image14.svg" style="width:6.26389in;height:3.19306in" />

Employer provision of training is important to ensure that courses are well tailored to the needs of firms and in turn London’s economy. Employers can provide either training that equips employees for the requirements of their current job (defined in the Employer Skills Survey as on-the-job training) or training that goes beyond that (off-the-job training). Despite easing recruitment pressures, employer investment in training has declined over recent years.

Since 2015, training provision by London employers has fallen by 6 percentage points (from 65% to 59%). Evidence suggests that a wide range of factors including rising costs pressures, constraints on employer capacity to plan and deliver training and the shift towards responsibility for training falling on individuals could be driving these trends. The overall trend in provision in London is similar to that for the whole of England.

<img src="attachments/skills/media/image16.svg" style="width:6.26389in;height:3.13194in" />

Online job posting numbers are a useful, albeit imperfect, indicator of real-time employer demand for labour. While the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic had an adverse impact on recruitment activity in early 2020, this reversed rapidly following widescale societal reopening and resurgent economic activity. However, between the latter half of 2022 and the beginning of 2025, job posting numbers trended steadily downwards and are now aligned with pre-pandemic levels. These trends reflect the gradual cooling and stabilisation of London’s labour market in the years following the pandemic.
